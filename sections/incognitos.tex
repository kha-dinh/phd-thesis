To bolster customers' trust in cloud, cloud providers are increasingly adopting secure hardware from compute vendors like Intel and AMD, which have secure primitives built into the silicon that can create a \gls{tee}.
The \gls{tee} protects sensitive data and programs from the rest of the platform, including compromised cloud administrators.

Unfortunately, even with hardware-provided isolation, side-channel attacks continue to undermine confidentiality guarantees of \gls{tee}.
The latest development of \gls{tee}-enabled hardware, \glspl{cvm}, incorporates the \gls{vm} with a thin \gls{tee}, also suffering from side-channel threats.
This chapter introduces \incognitos, a unikernel-based operating system that reimagines the OS as an active component contributing to the security of a \gls{cvm}, especially against side-channel attacks.

This chapter first explains the motivation for \incognitos, and how it establishes coherence between the \gls{os} protection and the one provided by \gls{cvm}-enabled hardware.
Then, it covers the design and evaluation of \incognitos, showing the evolution of traditional \gls{os} protection and enabling security and performance for \gls{cvm} workloads.

\section{Toward coherent OS-assisted CVM protection}


% The \gls{cvm} deployment model offers unprecedented protection for cloud workloads, enabling the deployment of existing (e.g., databases and web servers) and cutting-edge (e.g., AI inference and large language models~\cite{yifan2025pipellm}) workloads with minimal migration costs.
Despite their strong compatibility with existing worklodas,  \glspl{cvm} inherits existing side-channel attack challenges from the previous Intel SGX model while introducing unique challenges due to the expanded \gls{tcb}.

\hdr{Side-channel attacks}
In 1973, Butler Lampson~\cite{lampson1973confinement} described in his seminar work on the \emph{confinement problem}, the difficulties of preventing an untrusted program from leaking secrets through \emph{covert channels} or \emph{side channels}.
These channels, such as the time an operation takes, while not designed for information transfer, allow a program to communicate with other programs.



Side-channel attacks remain major challenges in modern computing systems.
Especially on cloud platforms where computation across thousands of users is shared on the same system, the \emph{shared} state between the victim and attacker processes becomes a major side-channel threat. 
Between different processes, the cloud \gls{os}/hypervisor multiplexes memory (through the page table), storage, and CPU execution time, while the hardware multiplexes microarchitectural states such as the cache and branch predictor.
These shared states are never designed to hold secrets; previous research has demonstrated the feasibility of microarchitectural attacks, such as cache attacks~\cite{yarom_flushreload_nodate,liu_last-level_2015}, to extract sensitive information, such as secret keys.

\glspl{tee} excabates the side-channel problem with a much stronger threat model compared to traditional protections (\cref{sec:intro:bg-os}).
The attackers in this threat model are the management software, i.e., the \gls{os} and the hypervisor. 
Thus, attackers are now given access to powerful capabilities like page access interception~\cite{xu2015controlledchannel} and interrupt timers ~\cite{sgx-step,bulck2018nemesis,cachezoom}, which they can leverage to leak information~\cite{controlled-channel} or amplify existing side channels~\cite{cachezoom,hahnel_high-resolution_2017}.

Side channels were a significant threat in Intel SGX and continue to persist in emerging \gls{cvm} models such as AMD SEV and Intel TDX, which remain vulnerable to a broad class of side-channel attacks.
(\autoref{cvm-side-channel} illustrates the side-channel attacks in \gls{cvm}.
Since the \gls{cvm} shares architectural and microarchitectural hardware states with the untrusted hypervisor, it suffers from similar side-channel attack vectors, such as page-table-based attacks (also called the \emph{controlled-channel} attack) and cache attacks~\cite{sevstep}.
Worse, newer \gls{cvm}-specific side-channels, such as the ciphertext side channel~\cite{li_cipherleaks_nodate,ciphersteal}, continued to be discovered.
Thus, research into side-channel mitigation for \gls{cvm} is a pressing need.

\hdr{Bloated TCB}
Another with the \gls{cvm} deployment model is the significantly bloated \gls{tcb}.
The \gls{tcb} of a \gls{tee} contains the components that are trusted to handle the user's secret.
The \gls{cvm} model now places the confidential application, along with all of its supporting software, within the trust boundary, placing a significant amount of code inside the TEE.
Specifically, the \gls{os} must now be a part of the \gls{tee}, which contains tens of millions of lines of code and thousands of known vulnerabilities.
This bloated \gls{tcb} significantly increases the attack surface.
First, since \gls{os} must interact with low-level hardware that was assumed trusted before the \gls{cvm} model, the shift in the trust model introduces easy attack vectors.
Research has demonstrated that the legacy \gls{os}, when deployed in a \gls{cvm}, exposes new attack surfaces, such as interrupt handling~\cite{wesee,heckler}.
Second, the large \gls{tcb} complicates efforts in defending against side-channel attacks; the \gls{os} itself, through complex interactions with the user applications, may \emph{amplify} existing side channels, or even \emph{introduce} new ones.




\hdr{\incognitos: coherent OS-assisted CVM protection}

To support more robust \gls{cvm} deployments, \incognitos is designed to address the above challenges.
It explores the principle of coherent protection (\cref{chap:intro}) by rethinking the traditional protection model of \gls{os} (\cref{chap:background}, \cref{sec:intro:bg-os}), to align with the protection established by \gls{cvm} hardware like AMD SEV and Intel TDX.

This shift in perspective has two implications.
First, this means that the \gls{os} must now incorporate side-channel attacks from untrusted hypervisors into its threat model.
\thename shows that this both presents challenges and opportunities \dash\ with the \gls{os}'s access to low-level capabilities like memory management and scheduling, \thename can \emph{efficiently} and \emph{transparently} protect the memory accesses from side-channel attack.
Second,  it motivates the exploration of alternative OS designs to achieve the security goals.
With a unikernel design, \incognitos render whole-system protection feasible, while also keeping the TCB minimal.

\section{Abstract}

Recent works have repeatedly proven the practicality of side-channel attacks in undermining the confidentiality guarantees of Trusted Execution Environments such as Intel SGX.
Meanwhile, the trusted execution in the cloud is witnessing a trend shift towards confidential virtual machines (CVMs).
Unfortunately, several side-channel attacks have survived the shift and are feasible even for CVMs, along with the new attacks discovered on the CVM architectures.
Previous works have explored defensive measures for securing userspace enclaves (i.e., Intel SGX) against side-channel attacks.
However, the design space for a CVM-based obfuscation execution engine is largely unexplored.

This paper proposes a unikernel design named \thename to provide full-system obfuscation for CVM-based cloud workloads.
\thename fully embraces unikernel principles such as minimized TCB and direct hardware access to render full-system obfuscation feasible.
\thename retrofits two key OS components, the scheduler and memory management, to implement a novel adaptive obfuscation scheme.
\thename's scheduling is designed to be self-sovereign from the timer interrupts from the untrusted hypervisor with its synchronous tick delivery.
This allows \thename to reliably monitor the frequency of the hypervisor's possession of execution control (i.e., VMExits) and adjust the frequency of memory rerandomization performed by the paging subsystem, which transparently performs memory rerandomization through direct MMU access.
The resulting \thename design makes a case for a self-obfuscating unikernel as a secure CVM deployment strategy while further advancing the obfuscation technique compared to previous works.
Evaluation results demonstrate \thename's resilience against CVM attacks and show that its adaptive obfuscation scheme enables practical performance for real-world programs.
