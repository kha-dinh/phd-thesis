The past century has witnessed rapid technological progress, with computing systems among its most transformative developments.
The advent of the Internet revolutionized global information exchange, while recent advances in artificial intelligence have accelerated scientific discovery at an unprecedented pace.
Underlying these breakthroughs are the infrastructures that enable computation—both software and hardware.
The \gls{os}, which mediates between them, remains a cornerstone of technological innovation.

Historically, the \gls{os} has served as the central authority for enforcing system security, providing a coherent protection model that governs users' access to resources.
However, advances in processor design have introduced new hardware-level security mechanisms, such as \glspl{tee}, that enforce protection models partially outside the \gls{os}'s control.
Consequently, system security is now jointly enforced by two distinct entities: the hardware and the \gls{os}.
When their respective protection models diverge, gaps or \emph{misalignments} may emerge, exposing attack vectors and undermining the intended guarantees of both.

This dissertation argues that hardware-assisted protection models must be systematically aligned with those established by the \gls{os}.
Building on this principle, I explore methods to \emph{retrofit} hardware security features into existing system architectures in a principled and coherent manner.
By restoring alignment between hardware and software protection, these methods enable systems to leverage the performance and security benefits of modern hardware while maintaining a unified protection model that is robust against modern threats.

\subsection{Limitations of existing OS protection}

Historically, the \gls{os} has been the central figure in ensuring systems security, enforcing a \emph{protection} model~\cite{lampsonprotection}.
This protection governs how programs access system resources, relying on long-standing abstractions such as processes to represent executing principals, and files to represent protected objects.
Combined with hardware support, these abstractions create two fundamental isolation boundaries: one separating user applications from the privileged kernel and another isolating processes from each other.

The traditional, process-centric \emph{protection} has served as the basis of OS security for decades, but its assumptions no longer align with modern computing environments.
Two key weaknesses are revealed by attempts to add security to modern systems.

\begin{itemize}
  \item First, the process-level isolation boundaries provided by the OS is no longer sufficient in containing bugs.
Contemporary software systems are increasingly large and complex—a peril underscored by the infamous Heartbleed vulnerability~\cite{heartbleed}, which exploited a bug in the OpenSSL cryptographic library.
These weaknesses call for the \emph{separation of privilege} within complex programs into smaller, isolated modules~\cite{capacity,erim,hakc,rlbox,privtrans,gudka2015clean}.
The coarse-grained, process-level protection of traditional \glspl{os} becomes a bottleneck for this decomposition, imposing significant development and performance costs~\cite{erim,capacity}.
\item
  Second, emerging \emph{confidential computing paradigms}~\cite{confidential,google-confidential-computing,sgx,kaplan_amd_nodate,cca} invert traditional trust hierarchies by placing the \gls{os} \emph{outside} of the trust boundary, necessitating a reconsideration of security goals.
Confidential computing, leveraging modern hardware features, establish \emph{secure enclaves} \dash\ containers that are isolated  from the rest of the system, even the OS or the hypervisor.
Such paradigm shift introduces a completely different trust model that existing OS protection were not designed to expressed.
Yet, previous systems retrofit security into this model, introducing attack surfaces such as the Iago attacks \cite{checkoway2013iago} that exploits the system call APIs to take control of secure enclaves.
\end{itemize}


Given these limitations, this dissertation calls for rethinking the legacy OS's protection model to better align with security and performance goals of modern systems.

\subsection{Rise of hardware-assisted protection}

At the same time, modern hardware increasingly implements its own protection and isolation mechanisms, often beyond the \gls{os}'s full visibility or authority.
Taking advantages of these new features, a plethora of systems has been developed and deployed, providing models of \emph{hardware-assisted protection}.
This thesis focuses on the two recent development in hardware-assisted protection: in-process isolation and VM-level confidential computing, or \gls{cvm}.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Domain compression.} In-process isolation (\autoref{inprocess-prot}) \emph{compresses} the protection domains inward to create \emph{sub-process isolation domains}, leveraging CPU features like Intel \gls{pku} that enable  userspace processes to establish memory isolation policies.
Works like ERIM and Hodor~\cite{erim,hodor} leverage these hardware extensions to created protected domain that isolate secret and contain the impact buggy code, showing significantly better performance than the process-level isolation.
\item \textbf{Domain expansion.} The \gls{cvm} paradigm (\autoref{cvm-prot}) \emph{expands} the protection domain outward from previous process level secure enclaves to entire VM.
This direction leverage recent advancement in TEE technologies introduced by Intel TDX, AMD SEV or ARM CCA to transform an entire VM, including applications and OS, into an protection domain, isolated from the untrusted hypervisor. 
Isolating the confidential application, along with all its supporting code and data, enables companies to deploy cutting edge workloads like LLM inference into \glspl{tee} without much adaption costs~\cite{confidential-llm}.
\end{itemize}



It is important that modern systems should take advantage of these new hardware features to improve their security and performance. 
Thus, exploring principles to securely incorporate these security features is the challenge for this dissertation.

\section{Approach}

I now describe the approaches used to tackle the challenge of adopting modern hardware security features to improve security and performance of systems.

\subsection{Retrofitting for security}
First, this dissertation argues that  the practical path to introduce security is \emph{retrofitting} \dash\ integrating security features into existing protection models without breaking compatibility.
This is also fit with the modern hardware features hardware features aim to provide compatibility.

Despite substantial advances in OS composition, OS protection has remained largely unchanged in mainstream \gls{os}, since the 1970s.
Numerous proposed systems that promised better security, like capability OS and microkernels, have not seen large-scale adoption. 
Modern applications continue to depend on long-standing standards such as \gls{posix}~\cite{posix} and the Windows API~\cite{winapi}, which constrain compatibility with alternative designs.
This status quo leads to a belief in the community that achieving security is a hopeless quest~\cite{hopelessquest}.
Ultimately, a majority of current computing infrastructures still rely on legacy protection.
On the other hand, there have been several successful securities retrofit that gained large adoption.
For example, SELinux~\cite{selinux} extends Linux’s \gls{dac} model with \gls{mac} enforcement, while Capsicum~\cite{watson2010capsicum} retrofits a capability-based \gls{api} in FreeBSD to enable fine-grained access control~\cite{freebsd-capsicum}.
Similarly, large applications such as Chrome and Firefox are retrofitting sub-process isolation to contain vulnerabilities from compromised third-party libraries~\cite{rlbox}.




\subsection{Coherent software/hardware protection}


While modern processors provide powerful protection primitives, 


their full security potential can only be realized when \glspl{os} integrate them in a coherent manner; in other words, protection enforced by the software (\gls{os}) must be coherent with the protection established with software support.

Previous research has demonstated that incoherency in the protection model is the bane of security.
Systems that leverage modern hardware features in an ad hoc manner, often reveal gaps in protections that undermine any benefits provided.
For instance, for in-process isolation systems, prior work has demonstrated \emph{confused deputy} attacks, arising from the \gls{os}'s lack of awareness of hardware-enforced protection domains~\cite{pku-pitfalls,jenny}.
Similarly, trying to retrofitting legacy \glspl{os} into VM-level isolation (e.g., AMD-SEV) can exacerbate side-channel and interface vulnerabilities, as demonstrated by recent attacks against confidential virtual machines~\cite{wesee,heckler,ciphersteal,li2021cipherleaks}.

This dissertation argues for aligning the protection model provided by the software hardware into a unified model. 
This can be achieve two complementary approaches:
(1) retrofitting security guarantees provided by hardware to the one provided by the SO, or (2) retrofitting the OS protection model to with reflect the one estalbished by hardware.
The work presented in this thesis, \capacity and \incognitos, examines two layers of the protection hierarchy to illustrate the broader principle of protection coherency. 


\begin{itemize}
  \item[-] \textbf{Retrofitting hardware to incorporate OS protection.}
	      At the intra-process level, \capacity demonstrates how intra-process domains established through modern ARM hardware acceleration can be coherently
	      retrofitted into legacy abstractions, with a novel cryptographic capabilities mechanism.
	      \capacity retrofits capability-based protection into legacy process abstractions, leveraging ARM Pointer Authentication (\gls{pa}) and Memory Tagging (\gls{mte}) to transform resource references into cryptographically verifiable capabilities.
	      This design enforces consistent security semantics across file and memory accesses, bridging the gap between software abstractions and hardware enforcementf
      \item[-] \textbf{Retrofitting OSes for protection of hardware domains}
	      At the system level, \incognitos explores how \glspl{os} can collaborate with hardware-enforced VM-level isolation to enforce coherent threat models, achieving performant and robust.
	      \incognitos redefines the role of the operating system under hardware-enforced isolation, treating it as an active participant in the threat model of trusted execution environments.
	      By coordinating scheduling and memory layout randomization, it mitigates side-channel leakage and strengthens enclave isolation.
\end{itemize}


Together, these systems demonstrate that harmonizing OS and hardware protection models yields architectures that are both more secure and more efficient-pointing toward a new design paradigm for hardware-conscious operating system security.
